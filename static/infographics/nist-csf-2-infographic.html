01How We Got Here
FEB 2013
Executive Order 13636
President Obama directs NIST to build a framework to improve critical infrastructure cybersecurity.
APR 2018
CSF 1.1
Minor update. Adds supply chain risk category and improves authentication guidance.
FEB 26, 2024
CSF 2.0 — Current
Major overhaul. New Govern function. All-sector scope. Stronger supply chain & governance guidance.
FEB 2014
CSF 1.0 Published
Initial release. Five core functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. Rapid adoption beyond critical infrastructure.
FEB 2022
Revision Process Begins
NIST opens public comment, workshops, and community engagement to build CSF 2.0.
02What Is It?
🗺️
Outcome-Based
Describes what you should achieve — not which products to buy or exactly how to implement controls. Technology-neutral by design.
🔓
Voluntary
Compliance is not legally mandated for most organizations. It is a best-practice framework, not a regulation.
🌐
Universal Scope
CSF 2.0 applies to all organizations — any size, any sector, any maturity level. Not just critical infrastructure.
🗣️
Shared Language
Bridges technical teams and executive leadership. Provides vocabulary for communicating cyber risk to any audience.
🔗
Maps to Other Frameworks
Official mappings exist to SP 800-53, ISO 27001, CIS Controls, NIST Privacy Framework, and others.
📊
Risk Management Tool
Designed to integrate with enterprise risk management (ERM) — treating cyber risk the same as financial or reputational risk.
03The New Govern Function
New in CSF 2.0
Govern (GV)
★ Most Significant Change in Version 2.0
Elevating governance from a subcategory to a core function signals that cybersecurity strategy, leadership accountability, and supply chain risk management must be treated as first-class organizational priorities — not technical afterthoughts. Govern establishes, communicates, and monitors the organization's cybersecurity risk management strategy and underpins all five other functions.
Organizational Context Risk Management Strategy Supply Chain Risk (SCRM) Roles & Responsibilities Policy Oversight
04The Six Core Functions
GV · GOVERN · NEW
Govern
Strategy, policy, accountability, and supply chain risk. Underpins all other functions — makes cybersecurity a business risk, not just a technical problem.
GV.OC — Organizational Context GV.RM — Risk Management Strategy GV.RR — Roles, Responsibilities, Authorities GV.PO — Policy · GV.OV — Oversight · GV.SC — Supply Chain Risk
ID · IDENTIFY
Identify
Understand assets, suppliers, threats, and risks. The foundation for everything else — you cannot protect what you don't know exists.
ID.AM — Asset Management ID.RA — Risk Assessment ID.IM — Improvement
PR · PROTECT
Protect
Safeguards to manage risk and prevent or reduce likelihood and impact of cybersecurity events.
PR.AA — Identity Mgmt & Access Control PR.AT — Awareness & Training PR.DS — Data Security · PR.PS — Platform Security
DE · DETECT
Detect
Find and analyze attacks and compromises in a timely manner. Closes the gap between breach and response.
DE.CM — Continuous Monitoring DE.AE — Adverse Event Analysis
RS · RESPOND
Respond
Contain and manage the effects of a detected incident. Speed and coordination determine damage.
RS.MA — Incident Management RS.AN — Incident Analysis RS.CO — Reporting & Communication
RC · RECOVER
Recover
Restore assets and operations. A mature Recover function means the organization returns to normal — or better — without catastrophic downtime.
RC.RP — Incident Recovery Plan RC.CO — Recovery Communication
05Implementation Tiers
1
Partial
Ad hoc and reactive risk management. Limited leadership awareness. Little integration with enterprise risk management.
2
Risk Informed
Leadership-approved risk practices, but not applied organization-wide. Some awareness of supply chain risk.
3
Repeatable
Formally approved policies. Consistent implementation across the organization. Supply chain risk managed.
4
Adaptive
Practices adapt based on lessons learned and predictive indicators. Continuous improvement embedded in culture. Active information sharing.

Tiers describe posture — not a maturity ladder to climb. A Tier 2 may be appropriate for a given organization's context and risk appetite.

06Organizational Profiles
Current Profile
Where You Are
Documents the cybersecurity outcomes your organization is currently achieving. An honest snapshot of existing posture — the baseline from which gaps are identified and priorities set.
Target Profile
Where You're Going
Documents the outcomes your organization aims to achieve, driven by mission objectives, risk tolerance, and regulatory requirements. Defines the destination for resource allocation and roadmapping.
↕️
The Gap = Your Security Roadmap The difference between Current and Target Profiles is not a failure — it is the prioritized work ahead. CSF 2.0 also introduces Community Profiles: sector-specific templates that allow organizations in the same domain to benchmark against a shared reference rather than starting from scratch.
07What Changed from CSF 1.1 → 2.0
🏛️
New Govern Function
Governance elevated from subcategory to standalone core function. Cybersecurity now treated as enterprise risk management, not just IT.
🌍
Universal Scope
Explicitly expanded beyond critical infrastructure to all organizations of any size or sector worldwide.
🔗
Supply Chain Emphasis
Significantly expanded supply chain risk management coverage, particularly within the Govern function, reflecting post-SolarWinds threat reality.
📋
Formalized Profiles
Organizational and Community Profiles formalized and expanded. Quick-Start Guides added for small businesses and new programs.
💡
Implementation Examples
Concrete examples added for every subcategory — showing what achieving an outcome looks like without prescribing the method.
🔤
Simplified Language
Clearer, more accessible writing throughout. Easier for non-technical executives and smaller organizations to engage with.
