01Legislative Origin
Why It Exists
Created in Response to FISMA (2002)
The Federal Information Security Management Act (December 17, 2002) directed NIST to develop security standards and guidelines for all federal information systems not designated national security systems. SP 800-53 — first published in 2005 — was the direct result: a comprehensive, structured catalog of controls that federal agencies could use to protect their systems and satisfy FISMA's requirements. It has since been revised five times to keep pace with evolving threats and technologies.

05Security Control Baselines (SP 800-53B)
Low Impact
~149
Controls Required
For systems where a breach would have limited adverse effects on operations, assets, or individuals. A foundational starting point.
Basic access control & authentication
Core audit & accountability
Incident response planning
Security awareness training
Moderate Impact
~287
Controls Required
For systems where a breach causes serious adverse effects — significant mission degradation, financial loss, or harm to individuals. Most common federal baseline.
Multi-factor authentication required
Continuous monitoring programs
Supply chain risk management
Advanced incident handling
High Impact
~370
Controls Required
For systems where a breach could cause severe or catastrophic harm — national security, critical infrastructure, or public safety at stake.
Maximum security & redundancy
Enhanced cryptographic protections
Stringent physical security
Full cyber resiliency controls

Control counts are approximate. Consult SP 800-53B for authoritative baselines. A separate Privacy Baseline applies to systems processing PII regardless of impact level.

SP 800-53 enters at Step 3 — Select. After a system is categorized (Step 2, using FIPS 199), the organization selects the appropriate baseline from SP 800-53 via SP 800-53B, then tailors it for their specific environment and risk profile. Controls are then implemented, assessed using SP 800-53A procedures, and continuously monitored throughout the system lifecycle. SP 800-53 works alongside SP 800-37 (RMF) — they are designed to be used together, not separately.
07Who Uses It & Why
🏛️
Federal Agencies
All U.S. civilian executive branch agencies must comply with SP 800-53 under FISMA. Agencies are expected to be compliant within one year of a major revision's publication date.
Required by Law
☁️
Cloud Service Providers
Providers seeking FedRAMP authorization must demonstrate compliance with SP 800-53 control baselines. Required to sell cloud services to federal agencies.
Required (FedRAMP)
⚙️
Defense Contractors
DoD contractors operating under CMMC (Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification) work with controls heavily derived from SP 800-53. Almost every CMMC control maps to an 800-53 control.
Required (CMMC)
🏥
Healthcare & Finance
Organizations in regulated industries voluntarily adopt SP 800-53 for its comprehensiveness. Its controls map to HIPAA, PCI DSS, GDPR, and other compliance requirements simultaneously.
Voluntary Adoption
🔬
Mature Security Programs
Any organization seeking a rigorous, comprehensive security baseline uses SP 800-53 to identify gaps. At 1,189 controls across 20 domains, it's among the most thorough frameworks available.
Voluntary Adoption
🌐
International Organizations
Since Rev. 5's expansion beyond federal scope, organizations globally use SP 800-53. Official crosswalks exist to ISO/IEC 27001:2022, allowing use alongside international standards.
Voluntary Adoption
