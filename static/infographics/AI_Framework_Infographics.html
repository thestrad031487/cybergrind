Voluntary Framework · January 2023

NIST AI Risk Management Framework

U.S. National Institute of Standards & Technology
Voluntary — no legal penalties
U.S. federal agency baseline
All sectors & org sizes
Four Core Functions
GOVERN
Establish org-level AI governance policies, roles, accountability structures, and culture of responsible AI.
MAP
Identify and analyze AI risk sources, affected parties, and potential societal and technical impacts.
MEASURE
Evaluate and quantify risks through bias detection, red-teaming, robustness testing, and benchmarking.
MANAGE
Respond to, monitor, and improve risk management. Accept, mitigate, transfer, or avoid identified risks.
Key Updates
2024
NIST-AI-600-1: Generative AI Profile
Released July 2024. Addresses unique risks from LLMs and generative systems — hallucination, synthetic content at scale, training data provenance.
2025
Domain-Specific Profiles
Sector profiles for healthcare, finance, and critical infrastructure turning high-level principles into practical, scenario-specific guidance.

Binding Law · August 2024

EU Artificial Intelligence Act

European Union · Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 · World's First Comprehensive AI Law
Legally binding
Extraterritorial reach
Phased enforcement 2024–2027
Risk Tier Classification
🔴
PROHIBITED
Banned outright. Social scoring, real-time public biometric ID, subliminal manipulation, scraping facial images.
🟠
HIGH-RISK
Strict pre-market compliance. Covers healthcare, HR, law enforcement, education, finance, migration, critical infrastructure.
🟡
LIMITED RISK
Transparency obligations only. Chatbots, deepfakes, AI-generated content must disclose AI involvement to users.
🟢
MINIMAL RISK
No specific obligations. Spam filters, AI-enabled video games, most consumer AI.
Enforcement Penalties

€15M or 3% revenue

Certifiable Standard · 2023

ISO/IEC 42001

International Organization for Standardization · First International AI Management Standard
Certifiable via third-party audit
Global applicability
Complements NIST & EU Act
Implementation Path
01
Gap Analysis
Evaluate existing AI policies against ISO/IEC 42001 requirements; identify overlapping controls and gaps.
02
Governance Structure
Appoint an AI governance lead; establish a cross-functional oversight committee aligned to the standard.
03
Risk Assessment
Conduct a formal AI risk assessment identifying risks to individuals, organizations, and society.
04
Technical Documentation
Compile documentation, risk management measures, and human oversight procedures for the AIMS.
05
Internal Audit
Verify the AI Management System (AIMS) functions as intended before engaging external auditors.
06
Certification Audit
Engage an accredited certification body qualified under BS ISO/IEC 42006:2025 for formal assessment.
07
Continual Improvement
Monitor model performance, conduct post-market surveillance, update AIMS regularly. Re-certification required.

Soft Law · 2019 · Updated 2023–2024

OECD Principles on AI

Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development · G20 Endorsed
Non-binding intergovernmental
Influenced EU AI Act & NIST RMF
First intergovernmental AI standard
Five Core Principles
1
Inclusive Growth & Well-Being
AI should benefit people and the planet. Address environmental impacts. Promote inclusive, sustainable development.
2
Human-Centred Values & Fairness
Respect rule of law, human rights, and democratic values. Non-discriminatory, privacy-protective AI.
3
Transparency & Explainability
Provide meaningful information about how AI systems work, especially when affecting people's lives and decisions.
4
Robustness, Security & Safety
AI systems must function reliably and safely throughout their lifecycle. Continuously assess and mitigate risks.
5
Accountability
AI actors are accountable for ensuring AI operates per these principles. Include mechanisms for redress when harm occurs.

Recommendation · November 2021

UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI

United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization · 194 Member States
Endorsed by 194 member states
Non-binding recommendation
Human rights-centered
Four Core Values
①
Human Rights & Human Dignity
AI must respect, protect, and promote human rights throughout its entire lifecycle — no exceptions.
②
Peaceful, Just & Interconnected Societies
AI should contribute to peace and justice, not be weaponized for surveillance or to suppress dissent.
③
Diversity & Inclusiveness
Develop AI for all of humanity — protect linguistic diversity, cultural pluralism, and marginalized communities.
④
Environment & Ecosystem Flourishing
Account for AI's environmental footprint — energy, resource extraction, and hardware throughout the AI lifecycle.
